Organizational Concepts for the Chamber

8 concepts derived from vertical transport, applied to the relay-lock

2.1 The Lock Sequence

1
ENTRY
Arrive from one state: night shift ends, last train missed, cargo delivered
2
SEALING
The chamber closes around you: shelter, warmth, information, safety
3
EQUALIZATION
The chamber adjusts your state: rest, food, connection to next mode, psychological decompression
4
LEVEL MATCH
You're now ready for the other side: first bus arrives, shuttle dispatched, cargo sorted
5
EXIT
Leave into the new state: home, next shift, hospital, delivery destination
The dwell time IS the architecture. The value is proportional to how well it manages the wait — not how fast it eliminates it.

2.2 Destination Dispatch

SCHINDLER PORT (2009)

Passengers enter destination on a touchscreen in the lobby. System groups people heading to similar floors, assigns a specific car. No buttons inside the car.

The key shift: intelligence moves from the cab to the lobby. The cab becomes dumb transport; the lobby becomes the smart interface.

CHAMBER APPLICATION

A night nurse finishes at CHUV at 01:30, needs to reach Renens. The chamber dispatches: "Shared ride with 3 others heading to Prilly/Renens. Bay 2, departing 01:45."

Pre-sorting eliminates the worst part of the dead window: not the wait itself, but the uncertainty of whether anything is coming.

2.3 Paternoster vs. Elevator

PATERNOSTER = Bus/Tram

  • Continuous — always moving, never stops
  • User synchronizes with the machine
  • Zero wait (but you must time your entry)
  • Low per-cabin, high aggregate capacity
  • 84 dep/hr at Vernier-Blandonnet

ELEVATOR = IC Train

  • On-demand — responds to calls
  • The machine synchronizes with you
  • Variable wait (press button, wait)
  • High per-cabin, lower aggregate capacity
  • Plan your trip around a specific departure

DEAD WINDOW (01:30–03:30 weekdays): Both systems stop. The paternoster goes silent and the elevator has zero cabs running. The weekday/weekend split is fundamental — Friday and Saturday, Noctambus provides a partial paternoster (38 trips, 9 routes). Monday through Thursday: nothing.

2.5 Relay Station Architecture

SWING STATION (quick handoff)

Under 1 minute. Minimal structure: a corral, a trough, a roof.

Corridor nodes: Geneva North (Node 2), Crissier-Bussigny-Ecublens (Node 5). Cargo transfer, logistics interface. Minutes, not hours.

HOME STATION (rest/recovery)

Every 100–160 km. Full program: bunkhouse, kitchen, stable, blacksmith.

Corridor nodes: Morges (Node 4), Montreux-Glion (Node 8). Night shelter, waiting, capsule rest. Hours — the full dead window.

CARAVANSERAI LOGIC

Relay/rest stations spaced one day's journey apart (~30–40 km). UNESCO inscribed 56 Persian caravanserais as a single network — not individual buildings.

9 chambers × ~11 km spacing = caravanserai spacing scaled for motorized travel. The value is in the system, not any individual node.

2.6 Threshold Chambers

AIRLOCK

Two doors, never both open simultaneously. Gradual pressure transition. Asymmetric timing: lock-in is fast (minutes), lock-out/decompression can take hours.

Chamber lesson: Entry is quick (night shift ends, you're in). Exit may require waiting. The asymmetry is the design.

NARTHEX

Threshold between secular exterior and sacred interior. Functions: climatic buffer, gathering space, psychological preparation, social filter.

Chamber lesson: The chamber prepares you for a state change. Leaving a night shift is not the same state as arriving home. The narthex is where the shift from "professional" to "personal" begins.

GENKAN

Japanese entrance. Ground-level foyer with a raised wooden platform. Shoe removal = ritual marking the boundary. Dates back 1,000+ years.

Chamber lesson: The GA tap-in COULD be a genkan moment — a ritual marking "you are now in the corridor's care." Currently invisible. The chamber could make this threshold legible.

2.7 Adaptive Architecture — Cedric Price

FUN PALACE (1961–1972, unbuilt)

A structural framework with daily reconfigurable components. "Behaviour would be unstable, indeterminate, and unknown in advance." Planned obsolescence — intended for dismantling after 10 years. Directly influenced the Centre Pompidou.

GENERATOR (1976–1979, unbuilt)

150 mobile 12'×12' cubes on a grid. The building "knew itself" and readjusted. Key innovation: the computer could "become bored and induce changes" if users weren't changing anything. First conceived "intelligent building."

CHAMBER APPLICATION

Time-adaptive: co-working by day, social space by evening, shelter by night, logistics interface at 03:00.

Programmatically uncertain: built for 10–15 years until the corridor's identity solidifies.

Self-reconfiguring: ridership patterns, shift schedules, and usage sensors drive reconfiguration.

2.8 Home Care — A New Transport Type (4th Circulation)

AVASAD + IMAD = 7,300 FIELD WORKERS

AVASAD (Canton Vaud, 5,000 employees) and IMAD (Canton Geneva, 2,300 employees). 90% field-based. IMAD confirms a dedicated EMD de nuit (night team) on route until 01:00.

NETWORK TRAVERSAL, NOT POINT-TO-POINT

Home care workers do not commute to a fixed workplace. They traverse — making 6–10 stops per shift across multiple communes. Insulin injections, wound care, palliative care checks.

CLOSEST ANALOGY: MULTI SYSTEM

Neither paternoster (scheduled loop) nor elevator (on-demand point-to-point). It is a traveling network traversal — autonomous agents with no fixed route. The chamber must accommodate waypoint logic: not origin-destination but pit stop where you resupply and continue.
