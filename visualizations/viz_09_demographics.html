02 — Age structure

The shape of two populations

Geneva's pyramid is broader in the 30-39 bracket — the peak working-age cohort drawn by international organizations and financial services. Lausanne bulges dramatically at 20-29: the university effect. Nearly 1 in 4 Lausanne residents is a student.

03 — Nationality

Half the city is from elsewhere

Geneva is nearly 50% foreign-born — the highest proportion of any major Swiss city. The top nationalities reflect its dual character: Southern European labor migration (Portugal, Italy, Spain) layered with a globally mobile professional class drawn by international organizations.

Lausanne shares the same top four nationalities in a different order. France leads here — proximity to the Vaud border and the university pipeline.

04 — Languages spoken at home

French dominates, diversity diverges

Both cities are overwhelmingly francophone, but Geneva's international sector creates a more polyglot environment. English, Portuguese, Spanish, German, and Italian all have significant presence — a linguistic diversity that mirrors the nationality profile.

05 — Economy & employment

Two service cities, different engines

Both cities are overwhelmingly tertiary-sector — over 94% in each. But Geneva's engine is international: finance, diplomacy, commodities trading, luxury. Lausanne's runs on education, technology, public administration, and an emerging biotech cluster around the EPFL Innovation Park.

Geneva has more jobs than residents. The 215,000 positions in a city of 209,000 are filled by 115,000 frontaliers and tens of thousands of domestic commuters — a labor market that extends deep into France.
06 — The invisible population

160,000 ghost citizens

Combined, Geneva and Vaud cantons absorb ~160,000 cross-border commuters daily — more people than the entire population of Lausanne. These frontaliers fill essential roles, pay taxes, and shape urban rhythms, yet they vanish from the corridor at 18:00 and appear in no diversity metric.

07 — Higher education

The academic corridor

Lausanne is the undisputed university city — 37,000 students represent 25% of the population. EPFL and UNIL together on the Dorigny campus create one of Europe's densest knowledge clusters. But Geneva carries serious academic weight too: UNIGE alone has 17,000 students, and the Graduate Institute (IHEID) is 93% international.

08 — Housing

Renters' cities

Both cities have exceptionally low homeownership by any standard — well below the Swiss national average of ~36%. Geneva's 0.34% vacancy rate is among the tightest in Europe. Finding an apartment here is a contact sport.

09 — The anchor contrast

Two cities, one corridor

The 101 km between Geneva and Villeneuve is not held together by geography. It is held together by two radically different anchor cities that, between them, generate the gravity that makes the corridor function.
