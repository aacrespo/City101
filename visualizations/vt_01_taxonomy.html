Taxonomy of Vertical Transport Systems

13 systems — what each teaches the relay-lock chamber

Sealed Box Systems

TRACTION ELEVATOR
Motor + sheave + counterweight. Steel ropes. Discrete trips, on-demand. Dwell 3–5s per floor. Industry wait target: 20s.
Chamber lesson: Sealed box, sensory disconnection. You enter one world and exit another. The transition is invisible.
HYDRAULIC ELEVATOR
Piston pushes from below. Low-rise only (max ~6 floors). No overhead machine room. Slower, simpler, cheaper.
Chamber lesson: Not every connection needs the same infrastructure. Some gaps are small and need simple solutions.
MACHINE-ROOM-LESS (MRL)
Motor embedded in the shaft wall — no dedicated machine room. Saves 10% building footprint.
Chamber lesson: The mechanism can be invisible. The chamber IS the machine. No "back of house" needed.
DOUBLE-DECK ELEVATOR
Two cabs stacked vertically, serving odd/even floors simultaneously. Passengers pre-sorted by floor parity in the lobby.
Chamber lesson: One shaft, two programs. Parallel processing of different user types in the same infrastructure. IC + S-Bahn = double-deck corridor.

Intelligent Dispatch

DESTINATION DISPATCH
Schindler PORT (2009). Passengers select destination BEFORE entering. System groups by destination, assigns a specific car. No buttons inside. Evolved from Miconic 10 (1992).
Chamber lesson: Pre-sorting changes the lobby from anxious to certain. The intelligence is in the dispatch, not the cab. Converts anxiety into scheduled certainty.
MULTI (ThyssenKrupp)
Rope-free, linear induction motor. Multiple cabins circulate in loops — up one shaft, across horizontally, down another. Each cabin autonomous. Prototype in Rottweil since 2017.
Chamber lesson: The building becomes a network, not a stack. Cabins are autonomous agents. Increases usable area 25–30%. Eliminates single-path constraints.

Continuous Flow

PATERNOSTER
Continuous chain of open compartments in a vertical loop. Never stops. User must jump on in sync with the machine. Invented 1866 (Peter Ellis).
Chamber lesson: Continuous flow vs. discrete trips — two fundamentally different philosophies. The user synchronizes with the machine, not the other way around. Bus = corridor's paternoster.
MOVING WALKWAY / TRAVELATOR
Horizontal belt or pallet system. Speed assist only. No enclosure, no threshold moment.
NEGATIVE example: Speed assist alone doesn't create a chamber experience. The chamber needs enclosure, transition, threshold.

Gradient Systems

FUNICULAR
Two cars on a single track, counterbalanced by cable. One goes up as the other comes down. Fixed route, fixed gradient.
Chamber lesson: The connection creates twin settlements. Valley town + hilltop town become functionally interdependent. When it stops at night, the hilltop is severed.
CABLE CAR / GONDOLA
Aerial transit over terrain that resists surface infrastructure. Cabins as mobile micro-rooms. 30,000 pax/hr in La Paz.
Chamber lesson: When the ground doesn't cooperate, you leave it. Each cabin is a miniature chamber in motion.
INCLINED ELEVATOR
Oblique movement — neither purely vertical nor horizontal. Follows terrain.
Chamber lesson: The real-world gap is rarely purely one axis. Nyon→Genolier is an inclined elevator problem.
RACK RAILWAY
Toothed rail engages a cogwheel for steep grades (up to 48%). Three tooth systems (Riggenbach, Abt, Strub) for different gradients.
Chamber lesson: The infrastructure must match the gradient. Different gaps need different teeth.

Threshold System

SHIP LOCK / CANAL LOCK
Chamber fills/empties to equalize water levels. Vessel enters, gates close, water level changes, gates open on the other side. 5 stages: entry → sealing → equalization → level-matching → exit.
Chamber lesson: The occupant is stationary; the SPACE transitions around them. Gates enforce directionality. The dwell time IS the mechanism.

Swiss Vertical Transport Precedents

LAUSANNE M2
Ouchy (373m) → Épalinges (711m). World's steepest metro. 338m rise in 6km. 12% max gradient. Fully automated.
Teaches: A short steep connection can unify a vertically stratified city.
FRIBOURG FUNICULAR
Running since 1899. Powered by wastewater — no motor. 2,700 litres fill a tank at top; weight difference drives the system.
Teaches: An economic necessity that became heritage. Light, gravity-driven intervention.
BERN MARZILI
105m of track, 1M pax/year, since 1885. Connects Aare river leisure zone to Bundeshaus institutional zone.
Teaches: A 105-meter "elevator" between two completely different urban programs.
GLION-ROCHERS-DE-NAYE
Montreux → Glion → summit. Funicular stops at night.
Teaches: Directly relevant — this IS Node 8. Creates the mountain↔lake split that defines the Montreux health ecosystem.

Hospital Vertical Transport — 3 Segregated Systems

1. STAFF CIRCULATION
Standard elevators, card-access restricted, shift-change peaks.
2. PATIENT / BED TRANSPORT
Oversized cabs (2.4m deep minimum), slow speed, smooth acceleration, priority override for emergencies (one button clears all other calls).
3. LOGISTICS
Clean supply goes up one shaft, dirty/waste goes down another. AGVs handle 300–2,000 carts/day. Pneumatic tubes at 7.6 m/s for lab samples, blood, pharmaceuticals.
KEY PRINCIPLE
These three circulations share a building but NEVER mix. Clean/dirty, urgent/scheduled, staff/patient — each has its own path. The healthcare corridor has the same three systems + a fourth circulation: home care workers who traverse the corridor in network patterns.
