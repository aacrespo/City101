# Circulation and Corner Construction Learnings

Findings from Cabin v3 review. Updated by Circulation Agent.

---

## 1. Stair width must account for stringer thickness

Clear width between stringers is what matters, not the overall stair footprint. In Cabin v3: stringers at x=37-42 (west) and x=122-127 (east) give 80cm clear width (122-42=80). Swiss code requires 90cm minimum for residential stairs. The fix is either wider stair opening or thinner stringers.

## 2. Bottom landing needs explicit planning from the start

The stair starts at y=45, only 7cm from the south stone wall interior face (y=38). A 90cm landing zone requires the stair to start at y=128 or the south wall to not be behind the stair. In a small cabin, this usually means the stair should not be placed hard against the short wall — it needs a landing zone or a turn.

## 3. Top landing zone is tight but workable with direction change

The top of the stair (y=518) has only 45cm to the north wall (y=563). Since the stair arrives against the west wall and the user turns east into the UF room, the perpendicular clearance (636cm east) compensates. Still, 90cm clear in front of the last riser is code-required in most Swiss cantons.

## 4. Corner posts must be modeled at frame wall intersections

When E/W walls butt into continuous S/N walls, a solid corner post (16x16cm minimum, same cross-section as the stud zone) must sit at the intersection. Without it, the insulation layer of one wall meets the interior face of the perpendicular wall with no structural element at the corner. The corner post provides:
- Structural continuity (vertical load path at the corner)
- Nailing surface for interior and exterior finishes
- Backing for the wind barrier return

## 5. Wind barrier must wrap corners — insulation must not be exposed

The most critical corner issue: the wind barrier (wood fibre board) on S/N walls runs their full 800cm length but stops at the end face. At x=0 and x=800, the assembly is "cut" — you can see the insulation edge. The wind barrier must wrap around onto the perpendicular face (return piece) to maintain the thermal envelope. Without this, convective air loops through exposed mineral wool destroy the insulation value at corners.

## 6. Transition elements at corners need explicit patches

The capillary break (bitumen) and thermal seal (compriband) run along each wall face but don't meet at the corners. The gap where S/N and E/W wall plates meet (roughly 15x10.5cm at each corner) must be patched with separate corner pieces. Without this, moisture can wick into the timber at the most vulnerable point — where two wall plates meet.

## 7. The stone ledge creates a perimeter seal problem

The 3cm stone overhang (stone projects beyond timber frame) creates a horizontal ledge at z=270. The transition elements sit on the wall plate (centered on the 36cm stone, at y=10.5-25.5 for south wall), but the timber cladding starts at y=3 (3cm from stone face). This leaves a 7.5cm horizontal gap between the cladding start and the transition seal where the floor cavity is exposed to the exterior. A flashing or drip edge is needed at this zone on all 4 faces.

## 8. Floor zone corners are easily overlooked

The floor assembly (gypsum ceiling, joists, OSB deck, screed, finish) starts inside the wall lines. At the building corners, the zone between the stone exterior face and the rim board / first joist has no floor elements — it's an open cavity visible from outside at the transition level. Blocking panels (timber, ~2.2cm thick) are needed at each corner to close this cavity.

## 9. Door clear width through stone walls

A door in a 40cm stone wall (36cm stone + 2cm render + 2cm plaster) creates a 40cm deep reveal. The door frame (8cm jambs) reduces the clear opening. In Cabin v3, the 90cm rough opening (x=350-440) with 8cm jambs gives 74cm clear — below the 80cm accessibility minimum. For a cabin, this may be acceptable, but for code compliance, the rough opening should be at least 100cm to achieve 84cm clear after frame.
