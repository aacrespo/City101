# 7. Implications

## For practice

The architect's role elevates. Not from designer to manager — that's the trajectory the profession has been resisting for decades. From coordinator to *system designer*. The architect who designs the organigram — who decides which domains are present, when, with what communication topology — is practicing direction générale as a design act, not an administrative function.

This changes what buildings are possible. Materials and construction methods that are currently too niche to propose — because the knowledge barrier is too high and the specialist too expensive to engage at sketch stage — become accessible to explore. The architect who can credibly propose rammed earth, cross-laminated timber, or bio-sourced composites at Phase 0 expands the design space for every project. The consequence is not that every building uses these materials. It is that the decision between concrete and rammed earth becomes a *design decision* — informed by buildability, cost, carbon, and experience — rather than a default dictated by the limits of accessible knowledge.

## For education

Organizational topology should be taught as a design discipline. Not project management — scheduling, budgeting, contract administration — but the design of the team structure that produces the building. Students should learn to design organigrams the way they learn to design plans: with attention to structure, flow, interface, and the consequences of each decision.

EPFL's Sentient Cities studio already operates in this territory. The explicit use of AI agents as design tools creates a natural laboratory for organizational design experiments. The process booklet — documenting how the work was organized alongside what was produced — becomes a record of organizational topology decisions with observable consequences. This is new pedagogical territory: teaching students that *who is in the room* is a design decision with design consequences, not an administrative given.

## For the profession

The cost collapse of early domain presence changes the economics of what buildings are achievable. When having structural, buildability, and sustainability knowledge present from Phase 0 is no longer a luxury reserved for well-funded offices, the baseline quality of architectural integration rises across the profession. Small firms gain access to the kind of multidisciplinary early-phase exploration that currently requires the overhead of a large practice.

And the relationship between architect and builder changes. If the architect arrives at the first meeting with the piseur already understanding — through agent-mediated exploration — the constraints of compaction reach, formwork sequence, and curing time, the conversation starts at a different level. The builder is not explaining basics. The builder is refining, correcting, adding the lived knowledge that no agent carries. The collaboration is denser because the starting point is higher. The craft is respected because it is understood — not as an obstacle to be managed but as a domain whose knowledge shapes the building from conception.

Constraint is generative, not decorative. That is what changes.
