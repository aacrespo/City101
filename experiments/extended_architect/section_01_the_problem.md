# 1. The Problem: Who Is in the Room

Architecture is an integrative discipline. The architect holds structure, envelope, program, sustainability, circulation, buildability, and experience — not as a checklist but as a simultaneous judgment. The quality of the building depends on how fully these domains are present when decisions are made.

Architects manage this complexity through layers. The process is not an attempt to hold everything in mind at once — it is a deliberate progression from broad to specific, each phase adding depth to what came before.

## Design by layers

The first layer is urban and programmatic. How do people arrive? What are the flows through the building and around it? Where does the building meet the ground, the street, the neighboring structures? The architect thinks in broad strokes — massing, orientation, access — and these early decisions set the frame for everything that follows.

The second layer is spatial. Walls appear. Rooms take shape. The plan becomes specific: where are the thresholds, the vertical circulations, the structural rhythm? The architect is now working at a finer grain, but still within the frame set by the first layer.

Then more layers accumulate. Structure becomes explicit — columns, spans, load paths. The envelope develops — insulation, cladding, openings sized for light and ventilation. Building systems enter — HVAC routing, electrical risers, plumbing cores. Each layer adds complexity, and each new layer can collide with a previous one. A duct route conflicts with a beam. A fire exit requirement reshapes the circulation that shaped the plan. The structural grid doesn't align with the facade module.

Resolving these collisions is where design time goes. The architect iterates: adjusting the plan to accommodate the structure, adjusting the structure to preserve the spatial intention, checking that the adjustment doesn't break the envelope logic. This process works. It produces complex, well-integrated buildings. It is how architecture has been practiced for centuries — through progressive layering and iterative resolution.

But it takes time. Each collision requires going back, understanding what conflicts, finding a resolution that satisfies both layers, and checking that the resolution doesn't create new conflicts downstream. The more layers, the more potential collisions. The more collisions, the more iteration. And the architect's ability to resolve collisions depends on understanding all the domains involved — which is where the limits appear.

## Two limits

**The cognitive limit** is not that architects can't handle complexity — they can and do. It is that certain domains are harder to access than others. An architect trained at EPFL understands structure, sustainability, materiality. The interdisciplinary ambition is real. But buildability — the craft knowledge of how a material is actually worked on site — is the domain furthest from the architect's training and the hardest to access without a specialist. When a collision involves buildability (the rammed earth wall needs formwork clearance that the plan doesn't show), the architect is less equipped to resolve it than when the collision is structural or spatial. So buildability gets deferred. "We'll resolve that in execution."

The cognitive limit is not about working memory or the number of things a human brain can juggle. It is about the *accessibility of domain knowledge*. Some domains — spatial, structural — are native to the architect's education. Others — craft, building systems, material science at the level of construction tolerances — are not. The layered design process handles the native domains well. It handles the non-native domains by deferring them to specialists who arrive later.

**The economic limit** determines when those specialists arrive. The Swiss SIA service model — Recommendation 112 and Regulation 102 — places specialist roles at the phases where their contributions produce contractual deliverables. The structural engineer's fee is budgeted against the preliminary design phase. The builder's knowledge is budgeted against execution. The sustainability consultant appears when the energy concept is due. Each specialist is engaged when their output is contractually required and their cost is justified.

This is rational coordination. It solves a real problem: managing complex projects with dozens of specialists under budget and schedule constraints. But it determines *who is in the room at each phase*, and who is in the room determines which collisions the architect can resolve early and which get deferred. The builder who knows that a rammed earth wall at this height requires formwork access from both sides — an 800mm clearance that reshapes the plan — arrives when the plan is done. The collision between buildability and spatial design, which could have been resolved in sketch phase, becomes a costly redesign in execution.

## BIM tried to close this gap

Building Information Modeling was, at its core, an attempt to make the communication between disciplines instantaneous. All specialists work on the same model. A change in the structural grid is immediately visible to the MEP engineer. A clash between a duct and a beam is detected automatically. The promise was that collisions would surface faster, reducing the time lost to sequential iteration.

BIM delivered on much of this promise. Clash detection works. Federated models allow multiple disciplines to see each other's contributions. The collision-resolution cycle compressed. But BIM did not change *who is in the room*. The same specialists still arrive at the same phases, for the same economic reasons. BIM made their communication faster once they arrived — it did not make them present earlier. The organigram remained unchanged.

## Conway's Law, stated for buildings

In 1967, Melvin Conway observed that organizations which design systems are constrained to produce designs which copy the communication structures of those organizations. Software engineering has spent decades operationalizing this insight — most recently through the "Inverse Conway Maneuver," where the team structure is deliberately designed to produce the desired system architecture. Want a modular system? Create small autonomous teams. Want tight integration? Co-locate everyone.

Architecture has no equivalent practice. The organigram — who communicates with whom, who is present at which phase, who holds authority over which decisions — is inherited from institutional convention, contractual default, or the SIA model's phase structure. It is drawn once, rarely revised, and its consequences for the building are treated as given rather than designed.

But if Conway's Law holds — and the evidence from both software and construction suggests it does — then the organigram is not an administrative artifact. It is a design decision with design consequences. The building is shaped by who was in the room when the layers were being resolved. Change who is in the room, and you change what collisions get resolved early, which domains are deferred, and ultimately, what the building becomes.

The question is how to change who is in the room without changing the economics that determine why they aren't there. That is what the next section addresses.
