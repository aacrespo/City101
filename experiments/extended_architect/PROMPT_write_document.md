## The thesis (do not lose this)
The architect's integrative capacity — holding multiple domain constraints simultaneously — can be extended by AI agents carrying domain knowledge. This collapses the cost of early multidisciplinary integration, turning the organigram from an administrative constraint into a design instrument. Consequence: buildings shaped by constraints that traditional practice encounters too late.

## Key framing decisions (from the 2026-03-19 conversation)
- This is about **cognitive extension**, not workflow optimization
- The brief is the brief. What changes is **who is listening from the start**
- The narrative/design argument isn't the objective function — it's what emerges when more domains are present. The space talks back differently
- Rammed earth is the go-to example: buildability must be present from conception because it dictates what's achievable
- EPFL already trains interdisciplinary architects. AI removes the structural limits (cost, time, cognitive ceiling) that constrain that training in practice
- Frame through "who is in the room" — not "how do we organize agents"
- Conway's Law: org structure → building structure. Extended: design the org structure to produce the desired building
- The proof of concept isn't a demo — it's six months of City101 + the Lock builds. The practice IS the proof

## Writing approach
- One author voice, clean prose. Not academic-stiff but not casual either. The tone of "From Codebase to Corridor" is the reference.
- Lead with claims, then evidence. Not "we observed X therefore Y" but "Y. Here's why: X."
- Use concrete examples over abstract frameworks. The rammed earth wall, the archipelago finding, the 709 objects, the 42-fold frequency variation.
- Diagrams and tables where they compress information. Prose where argument matters.
- Each section should be writable independently (so you can do one per session if needed).
