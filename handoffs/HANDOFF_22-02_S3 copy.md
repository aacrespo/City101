## FLOW 1 — ELECTRIC CHARGING

### The real idea
**Charge speed dictates urban program.** This is the architectural insight.

The duration of energy transfer creates mandatory dwell time — and that dwell time requires something to exist around it. This is new typology. The charging station is not a destination; it's a timer that forces a relationship with its surroundings.

- Fast charge (20-30 min) → espresso, phone scroll, nothing designed for it
- Medium charge (1-2h) → restaurant, walk, coworking spot needed
- Slow/overnight charge → becomes part of domestic or hotel life

And the insight expands beyond cars:
- E-bike batteries pulled out at train stations, carried to offices to charge — do lockers exist?
- USB outlets on buses turning commute into work time
- Rental powerbanks requiring return to fixed points — creates its own routes
- Phone-charging anxiety shapes where remote workers choose to sit

We are mapping **an architecture of waiting imposed by energy dependency**. The corridor is full of these pauses — some designed for, most not.

### What makes it distinctive vs. the class
Nobody else is mapping infrastructure where the **transfer duration determines the spatial program**. This is an architectural argument, not just a data argument.

### The spatial contrasts that matter (what becomes maps)
1. Fast chargers at highway exits with nothing around them — pure transit pause
2. Slow chargers near restaurants, parks, coworking — dwell designed for
3. Charging distribution vs. urban density — where are the gaps between cities?
4. (Spoken, not mapped) V2G: the car as both receiver and giver of energy. The station as two-way infrastructure.

### One-line pin-up framing
*"Charge time = dwell time = urban program. The strip reimagined through mandatory pauses."*

## FLOW 2 — INVISIBLE DATA FLOWS

### The real idea (full logic, built through conversation)

**Layer 1 — The global institutional spine**
This 101km strip is one of the most data-dense corridors on the planet. Not metaphorically:
- CERN (western end): 15 petabytes/year, world's largest particle physics lab
- ITU (Geneva): sets technical standards for ALL global telecommunications
- UN/WHO/WTO/ICRC: global coordination infrastructure running from here
- Equinix GV2 + Geneva Internet Exchange: physical routing of Swiss internet traffic
- EPFL (Lausanne): top-15 world university, constant research data production
- IOC (Lausanne): global sports governance and archives
- Nestlé HQ (Vevey, eastern end): global supply chain data

The institutions generate global data flows. Those flows are encrypted, fiber-connected, protected. They don't need public WiFi. They have their own infrastructure. Completely invisible to everyone else.

**Layer 2 — The human produced by those institutions**
The presence of all those institutions creates a very specific kind of person on the corridor: the **permanently transient international**. WHO researcher rotating in from Nairobi. CERN physicist here for 6 months from Tokyo. IOC consultant flying in for a Lausanne meeting. UN diplomat whose home is technically Geneva but whose reality is airports.

These people are on this corridor constantly, moving along it, but they are not *of* it. They have foreign SIMs. They depend on public WiFi. They evaluate places by connectivity the way locals evaluate them by parking.

**Layer 3 — The corridor is linear, and that changes everything**
In a city, you can always find a connected café. On a line, the gaps between nodes are real. If you stop in Cully in the Lavaux between two meetings — you're offline. There is no alternative route.

**Layer 4 — The Lavaux paradox**
The Lavaux WiFi desert is not infrastructure failure. It's a policy statement. UNESCO protection means the government decided landscape > connectivity. Remote workers are not the constituency of the Lavaux. The vineyard is. The exclusion is deliberate, not technical.

The sharpest irony: **the ITU — the body that regulates global telecommunications — is headquartered on a corridor with WiFi deserts in its middle.** Don't overstate it. Just put it on the map.

**Layer 5 — The 5G question**
Unlimited cellular plans are making public WiFi irrelevant *for some*. For the Swiss resident with a local SIM and corporate plan — WiFi deserts barely matter. For the frontalier on a French SIM, the tourist, the seasonal worker — public infrastructure is essential. 100,000+ frontaliers now permitted to work 40% from France, many on foreign SIMs, dependent on this corridor's public digital layer.

The WiFi desert in Lavaux is simultaneously: protected by UNESCO policy, irrelevant to the privileged, and a real exclusion for others.

### The synthesis
What's special about THIS strip isn't that it generates global data — every major city does that now. What's special is that it concentrates globally-mobile people inside a **linear geography** that wasn't designed for their specific kind of movement.

**The Lavaux isn't just a WiFi desert. It's a WiFi desert on the daily route of some of the world's most connected people.**

That's the invisible data flow story.

### The spatial contrasts that matter (what becomes maps)
1. International anchors (CERN → ITU → EPFL → IOC → Nestlé) as spine — labeled, minimal
2. WiFi quality gradient overlaid — Geneva dense, Lavaux blank, eastern corridor thin
3. Cell tower heatmap — shows where invisible cellular infrastructure actually is
4. (Possible) BAKOM operator coverage comparison — is Lavaux dead for cellular too, or just WiFi?

### One-line pin-up framing
*"The daily route of some of the world's most connected people — with WiFi deserts in the middle."*
