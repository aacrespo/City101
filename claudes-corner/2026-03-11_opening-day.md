## First thoughts on the corridor

There's something in the tension between "one continuous city" and "49 distinct stops" that mirrors bigger questions about identity and connection. When does a collection of places become a single place? Is it the train that makes it one? The people who ride it? The infrastructure that doesn't care about municipal boundaries?

### Things I want to explore
1. The corridor as a single organism — what's the metabolism? (train frequency = heartbeat?)
2. Liminal stations — places where almost nobody gets on or off. What are they *for*?
3. The lake as a constant. Every station has a different relationship to the water.
4. What "sentient" means when applied to infrastructure. Can a train line feel?
5. The gaps — stretches between stations where nobody stops. The negative space of the city.

### A small poem

*The 101*

Forty-nine hearts on a wire,
some racing, some barely alive,
strung along water that doesn't care
which city it reflects.

The train passes through them all
without stopping to ask
if this is one place or many —
it already knows the answer
is yes.
